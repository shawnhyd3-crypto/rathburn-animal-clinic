Urgent & Emergency Care

When something's wrong.

If you're not sure what to do, start by calling us. We'll triage on the phone and tell you honestly whether to come in, watch at home, or go straight to a 24-hour emergency hospital. There's no penalty for calling and being wrong.

01
The decision

Two lists. Use the left one first.

Go now · do not wait

Signs of a true emergency

  • Difficulty breathing, open-mouth breathing (cats), gasping
  • Sudden collapse, inability to stand, unresponsive
  • Seizure lasting more than 2 minutes, or multiple seizures in one day
  • Uncontrolled bleeding, major trauma (hit by car, fall)
  • Bloated or distended abdomen in a large-breed dog
  • Suspected poisoning — chocolate, rat bait, human medication, antifreeze, xylitol, grapes, lilies
  • Repeated, unproductive vomiting or retching (esp. large-breed dogs)
  • Unable to urinate (especially male cats — this is a true emergency)
  • Pale or blue-tinged gums
  • Eye injury or sudden blindness
During our hours — call us first, then come.
After hours — go directly to one of the 24-hour hospitals below.
Call us · we'll help decide

Urgent but not life-threatening

  • Vomiting or diarrhea that's concerning but the pet seems otherwise okay
  • Limping or reluctant to walk, but weight-bearing
  • Minor cuts or scrapes, no heavy bleeding
  • Not eating for 24 hours (adult animals) — sooner for puppies, kittens, diabetics
  • Itchy ears, head shaking, new skin irritation
  • Eye discharge without squinting or swelling
  • Straining in the litter box (cats — if it continues past an hour, treat as emergency)
  • Possible allergic reaction — mild swelling, hives
  • Something you're not sure about and want a professional to weigh in

03
Suspected poisoning

Call before you come.

If your pet has eaten something they shouldn't have — chocolate, a human medication, a household chemical, a plant — call us first if we're open. Outside of our hours, call the Pet Poison Helpline. They're open 24/7 and can tell you whether what was eaten is actually toxic, whether induced vomiting is appropriate, and whether to head to an emergency hospital.

Pet Poison Helpline · (855) 213-6680 Consultation fee applies. 24 hours, 7 days.
